For patient records and treatment history, I selected a relational database such as PostgreSQL. This choice ensures transactional integrity, fast updates, and compliance with healthcare regulations. ICU monitoring devices, however, require a different approach, so I included a streaming system like Kafka or AWS Kinesis to ingest real‑time vitals continuously. These streams are stored in a data lake for raw archival and also fed into a data warehouse for aggregated reporting. To support natural language queries such as “Has this patient had a cardiac event before?”, I added a vector database like Pinecone or Weaviate. This allows embeddings of doctor notes and patient history to be searched semantically, enabling doctors to query in plain English. Finally, for management dashboards, a data warehouse such as Snowflake, BigQuery, or Redshift provides structured, optimized reporting on occupancy and costs.

The OLTP boundary lies at the relational database and streaming ingestion layer. Here, patient records, billing transactions, and ICU vitals are captured in real time with strict consistency. Once data is extracted, transformed, and loaded, it moves into the OLAP boundary. This includes the data warehouse, vector database, and analytics layer. OLAP systems are optimized for queries, aggregations, and predictions rather than fast inserts. In this design, OLTP ensures reliable day‑to‑day operations, while OLAP enables advanced analytics, AI models, and reporting. The clear separation ensures transactional workloads do not slow down analytical queries, and vice versa.

A significant trade‑off in this design is complexity versus flexibility. By using multiple specialized systems—relational databases, streaming platforms, data lakes, warehouses, and vector databases—the architecture becomes harder to manage and requires skilled staff. However, this diversity is necessary to meet the hospital’s four distinct goals: transactional integrity, real‑time monitoring, semantic search, and management reporting. To mitigate complexity, I would adopt a lakehouse approach where possible, integrating lake and warehouse layers into a unified platform such as Databricks or Delta Lake. This reduces duplication, simplifies governance, and lowers costs while still supporting diverse workloads. Additionally, strong data governance and standardized ETL pipelines would ensure smooth integration across systems.

In summary, the chosen architecture balances transactional reliability with analytical power. Relational databases and streaming systems handle patient and ICU data, while warehouses and vector databases enable AI predictions, natural language queries, and management dashboards. The OLTP–OLAP boundary is clearly defined, and although complexity is a trade‑off, it can be mitigated through lakehouse integration and governance practices.
