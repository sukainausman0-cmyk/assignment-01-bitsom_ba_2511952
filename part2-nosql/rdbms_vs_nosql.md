## Database Recommendation

When designing a patient management system for a healthcare startup, my first instinct is to prioritize consistency, reliability, and trustworthiness of data. Patient records involve sensitive information such as medical history, prescriptions, and billing details. For this type of workload, I would recommend using MySQL, because it follows the ACID principles: Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, and Durability. These properties ensure that every transaction is reliable, that updates are consistent across the database, and that no partial or corrupted data can slip through. In healthcare, where even a small error could affect patient safety, this level of rigor is essential.

MongoDB, on the other hand, follows a BASE philosophy (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent) and is guided by the CAP theorem, which emphasizes trade‑offs between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance. MongoDB excels when data is unstructured, fast‑changing, or needs to scale horizontally across servers. For example, product catalogs or social media feeds benefit from this flexibility. However, in a patient management system, eventual consistency could mean that two doctors see different versions of the same patient record at the same time, which is unacceptable.

That said, if the startup also wants to add a fraud detection module, my recommendation would evolve. Fraud detection often requires analyzing large volumes of logs, transactions, and behavioral patterns in real time. MongoDB’s flexible schema and ability to handle diverse, high‑velocity data make it well suited for this purpose. In that scenario, I would suggest a hybrid approach: MySQL for the core patient management system, ensuring strict ACID compliance, and MongoDB for the fraud detection module, enabling scalable and rapid analysis. This way, the startup benefits from the strengths of both systems without compromising patient safety.
